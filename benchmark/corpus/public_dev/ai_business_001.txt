Price discrimination is often described as a method for extracting consumer surplus, but its welfare effects depend on market structure and access. A student subscription illustrates the ambiguity. The lower price can bring a software service within reach of users who would otherwise leave the market. At the same time, the firm may use eligibility rules and switching costs to separate customers whose willingness to pay differs for reasons unrelated to production cost.

Suppose marginal cost is close to zero and monthly demand in two segments is Q1 = 120 - 4P and Q2 = 80 - 2P. A uniform price forces the seller to balance both demand curves, while segmented prices allow separate revenue-maximizing choices. The calculation may increase total output, but output alone does not establish fairness. Verification burdens, privacy costs, and the possibility of excluding students without standard documents also matter.

The policy question is therefore not whether differential pricing is inherently good or bad. It is whether the mechanism expands access without creating disproportionate surveillance or deceptive renewal practices. Regulators can require clear eligibility rules, simple cancellation, and limits on secondary use of verification data. Universities can also negotiate institutional access when individual screening would impose avoidable administrative costs.

A defensible evaluation should report several outcomes: participation by income group, average price, cancellation friction, data collected per verification, and the share of rejected applicants who later prove eligible. These measures reveal distributional effects that a single profit or consumer-surplus estimate cannot show. The strongest recommendation is conditional: permit targeted discounts when access gains are measured and privacy safeguards are auditable, but revisit the program if exclusion errors or data reuse grow.
